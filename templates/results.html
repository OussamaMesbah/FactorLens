Context from "Superstar Investors" (Brooks, Tsuji & Villalon, 2019)
Berkshire Hathaway (Buffett)
Dominated by Value (HML), Quality (QMJ), and Low-Risk (BAB) exposures. Berkshire's cheap insurance float essentially acts as leverage on these factors, amplifying returns without proportionally increasing risk — "buy quality merchandise when it is marked down."
PIMCO Total Return (Gross)
Primarily explained by the bond market factor (duration) augmented by credit factor exposure. The AQR paper shows that Gross's famous duration timing added minimal value on average; the persistent credit exposure was the true driver of excess returns.
Quantum Fund (Soros)
Strong Trend/Momentum factor across equities and currencies. Soros's market timing (dynamic beta) added positive alpha in some periods. The fund's high alpha beyond systematic factors is the hardest to replicate.
Magellan Fund (Lynch)
Significant Size (small-cap), Momentum, and Market exposure. Part of Lynch's record came from taking more risk (higher beta) and harvesting the small-cap premium — not purely from stock selection, though alpha was notably positive.

Key takeaway: Systematic factor exposures explain the majority of returns of even the world's most celebrated investors. The replicating portfolio you see here captures those systematic tilts using low-cost, liquid ETFs. Any residual alpha — the intercept in the regression — represents skill or genuinely unique insight beyond replicable factors.
